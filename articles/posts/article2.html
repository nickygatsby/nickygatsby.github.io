Joint Valuations

Published on June 3, 2026

Moving from textbook DCFs to real-world modeling, and why a "joint valuation" brings the whole picture together.

There is a big difference between reading about a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model in a textbook and using a live valuation model at a financial institution. Recently, I had an opportunity to study an organization's master valuation model for a network of consumer lending branches. When you're staring at a spreadsheet of delinquency rates, pre-tax net income and fixed assets, it's initially overwhelming. But as I worked through each formula and began to follow the logic behind each number, it started to make sense. What really clicked for me was that I had finally seen a true example of how the concept of "joint valuation" works.

I will be breaking down the conceptual mechanics of how a consumer lending branch gets valued, and illustrate the importance of viewing that valuation from multiple perspectives.

The Engine of a Consumer Lender

To truly understand the valuation, you have to understand the business model behind the lending side of a financial institution. In contrast to a software company that sells subscription-based products, a consumer lender's "product" is money. In essence, when valuing a consumer lending branch, you are placing a value on the branch's living and breathing portfolio of debt.

Looking at just one method or factor of valuation could be dangerous as you essentially ignore important data, leading to false numbers.

In order to arrive at a final value for a business opportunity, the model of analysis that I was working on involved using a "Joint Valuation Approach" — meaning, the combination of the Asset Method and the Earnings Method in order to "triangulate" an accurate and defensible value. Here’s how all of the moving parts fit together conceptually:

1. Asset Method

The second piece of this valuation method looks at the branch as a profit-generating entity moving forward.

Valuing the Portfolio: Because not all loans are alike, the model separates loans into "good" loans (the borrower is 30 days or less late on payments) and "bad" loans (the borrower is 90 days or more late on payments). "Good" loans are valued a fair value premium (1.15x) and are depreciated or highly discounted as those borrowers are most likely to pay back. For those 60 days late or even 90 days late have extremely lower rates as they unlikely to pay their loans back, therefore, causing charge offs.

Net Asset Value: After determining the value of the loan portfolio based on the criteria previously mentioned, you then add the value of the branch’s cash and fixed assets and then subtract their total liabilities in order to arrive at the final Asset Value.

2. Earnings Method

The first half of the valuation process looks at the balance sheet. Assuming that the branch ceased making any new loans as of tomorrow and proceeded to only collect the money that was owed to them — what would the branch be worth?

While no longer considering only loans, this methodology now considers Trailing Twelve Months (TTM) Pre-Tax Net Income generated by the branch. After taking the actual earnings of the branch and applying a market multiple, you arrive at an estimate for what a buyer would pay to "own" the right to receive the future cash flow that the branch will generate.

3. Blend- Joint Valuation

Now that you have completed both Asset Valuation and Earnings Valuation, which amount is "right?"

Answer: both. By utilizing both methods equally, or 50/50, you have arrived at the "Hybrid" Value or the total picture of the Branch. The Hybrid Value contains both the factual, tangible value of the existing loan portfolio while accounting for the ability of the branch to continue operating and generating future income.

Takeaway?

What I learned about finance has led me to believe that being a math genius is not as important as knowing the business's story. Each part of the financial model represents an action that can be expressed by measuring the world: changes made with interest rates, marketing dollars spent and timing of consumers' payments received.

Understanding how the "joint valuation" of all these previously separate operational stories can be combined into one number is both rewarding and enlightening because of the way these stories are combined into one piece in the finance world.
